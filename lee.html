Anthony van
Leeuwenhoek

Discoverer of Germs, Develops the Microscope

Nearly three centuries have passed since Leeuwenhoek, of Holland first gazed upon a mysterious new world of tiny microbes that no one had ever seen before.Many of these germs were friendly, others were among the worst enemies of the human race.Not even in the wildest dreams did Leeuwenhoek imagine the importance of the wornderful world of germs he had just discovered.Humanity had to wait two hundred years until the days of Pasteur before the full meaning of this strange new world of germs could be fully realized.Millions of people were still to die from diseases that might have been prevented had the full importance of Leeuwenhoek's discoveries been recognized.Scientists of those days were busy with other matters in the realm of chemistry,physics and astronomy before they turned their attention to finding the causes of the world's most devastating diseases.Leeuwenhoek was not a trained scientist.He ran a dry-goods store.But in his spare time he developed great skill in grinding tiny lenses by hand.It was tedious work, but he was highly successful.He was a man without any formal education,which was rather fortunate, for his ignorance of Latin saved him from foolish philosophies so prevalent at that time.He had to depend on his own observations, and in simple Dutch style he exploded some of the world's most cherished myths and theories.To Leeuwenhoek more than to any other man we probably owe the very beginnings of the microscope.This valuable instrunment has probably contributed more information than any other used in the study of the human body.Leeuwenhoek's microscopes were crude, often consisting of one carefully hand-ground lens.For suitable lighting he had to hold them up to the window.But His discoveries were astounding to men of science in those days.And from his humble beginning has come a vast amount of knowledge concerning the human body and the diseases to which it falls heir.
